# Aesthetic Judgment

Taste is not preference with better vocabulary. It is perception under compression: hundreds of small discriminations collapsing into one decision that feels obvious only after someone has made it. Most people can explain why something works once it exists. Fewer people can sense what is wrong while the thing is still formless.

Filmmaking made this real for me because film is brutally sensitive to judgment. A cut changes causality. A frame decides moral distance. Silence can make an image feel honest, vacant, sacred, manipulative, or cheap. Two shots placed beside each other can produce a thought that neither shot contains alone. Timing is not decoration; it is meaning moving through duration.

That is why taste matters. Not because things should be prettier, and not because polish is a substitute for substance. Taste is the faculty that knows when more explanation makes something smaller. It knows when a rough edge is texture and when it is incompetence. It understands that attention is physical: people feel pacing before they can justify it, trust before they can name it, confusion before analytics can diagnose it.

Software has the same problem, though it often hides behind functionality. A product can satisfy the checklist and still feel dead. It can have the right features, the right flows, the right technical architecture, and still fail because nothing in it has rhythm. The user is not only completing tasks; they are being moved through a sequence of assumptions. The interface is always directing attention, even when it pretends to be neutral.

As intelligence becomes more common, aesthetic judgment becomes more important, not less. If everyone can generate competent text, images, code, decks, and interfaces, the scarce act shifts from production to selection. What should exist? What should be deleted? What deserves emphasis? What sequence makes the thing legible? What is technically correct but spiritually off? Generation increases the volume of possible artifacts; taste decides which ones are allowed to become real.

This is where filmmaking becomes more than a personal anecdote. It teaches the discipline of framing. It teaches that omission is an argument. It teaches that every surface has a tempo, every transition has ethics, and every detail either clarifies the whole or quietly taxes it.

Good judgment is not loud. It usually appears as restraint. The unnecessary line removed. The second before the cut. The interface that does not explain what the design already made obvious. The product that feels inevitable because someone absorbed enough ambiguity to prevent the user from carrying it.

Aesthetic judgment is the ability to make intelligence felt.
